# Project Overview

This project studies the causal effects of high schools on students' later higher-education outcomes in Chile, with a central focus on major choice and especially STEM enrollment. The core question is whether schools affect those outcomes only through academic achievement, such as standardized test scores, or also through non-score channels like preferences, information, exposure, or school-specific culture.

The empirical strategy relies on Chile's centralized school assignment system. The idea is to use the quasi-random variation generated by the assignment mechanism, together with computed assignment probabilities, to estimate school-specific causal effects. The current reduced-form plan is to estimate school effects on math test scores and on STEM enrollment while controlling for assignment risk and predetermined covariates, then use schools with near-zero score effects to isolate evidence of non-score channels in major choice.

The project also includes a structural component. That part models students' higher-education field choices using ranked preference lists, allowing utility to depend on distance, test scores, covariate-cell heterogeneity, and school-field preference shifters. In that framework, the school-specific preference shifters are the key objects for testing whether schools directly shift preferences over fields even after flexibly accounting for achievement.

An important technical pillar of the project is replicating the Chilean assignment mechanism closely enough to simulate counterfactual assignments and compute school-level assignment probabilities. Progress here appears substantial: the current draft reports replication of about 99.47% of grade-9 assignments for roughly 80,000 students in 2018, despite institutional details that go beyond textbook deferred acceptance, including reserved quotas, guaranteed origin-school admission, sibling-linked applications, and transition-period rules.

At this stage, the project seems to be in a strong design-and-implementation phase rather than a final-results phase. The conceptual framework is well developed, the reduced-form and structural approaches are both laid out, and the mechanism replication work is advanced. The main next steps are to close the remaining assignment-replication gap, extend that success to later cohorts, compute reliable assignment probabilities, and then move into estimation of school effects on achievement and major choice.
