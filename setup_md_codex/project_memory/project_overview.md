# Project Overview

This project studies the causal effects of high schools in Chile on students' later higher-education outcomes, with a central focus on major field choice and academic achievement. The main objective is to estimate how much schools causally affect test scores and how much they causally affect where students apply, enroll, and persist in higher education, especially across fields such as STEM.

The project is motivated by the idea that standard outcomes such as college enrollment or number of semesters are too coarse to capture meaningful differences across schools. In this setting, schools may affect not only whether students attend college, but also the selectivity and labor-market value of the programs they target, the fields they choose, and whether they later switch programs or institutions. A central contribution of the project is therefore to study higher-education choices at the program-institution level rather than relying only on broad enrollment measures.

The empirical strategy relies on two linked centralized systems in Chile. First, the school assignment system generates quasi-random variation through lottery tie-breakers in oversubscribed high schools. Second, the higher-education application system centralizes ranked applications to program-institution pairs, making it possible to observe application portfolios, admissions-related score components, enrollment, and later switches with unusual granularity. The current design aims to estimate school-specific causal effects for oversubscribed schools at a large scale while controlling for assignment risk and predetermined covariates.

The project is currently reduced-form. It estimates school effects on outcomes such as higher-education entrance-exam scores, exam-taking choices, STEM content of application portfolios, field-level application and enrollment outcomes, and related measures of college quality or expected returns.

An important technical pillar of the project is replicating the Chilean assignment mechanism closely enough to simulate counterfactual assignments and compute assignment probabilities. This is not a textbook deferred-acceptance environment: quotas, guaranteed admission in origin schools, and other institutional features matter empirically. This replication exercise is a core input into the risk-controlled estimation strategy.

At this stage, the project is in a strong design-and-implementation phase rather than a final-results phase. The main next steps are to finalize the assignment-probability machinery, extend the replication and estimation workflow across cohorts, and then produce school-level causal estimates for achievement and higher-education field choice. Distinguishing achievement and non-achievement channels remains substantively interesting, but it is currently a secondary extension rather than the main headline of the project.
