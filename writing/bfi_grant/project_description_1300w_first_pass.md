# Project Description First Pass

Word count: 1162

Education is one of the central investments through which societies build human capital. Yet we still know surprisingly little about what, exactly, high schools do. Do students who attend “better” schools achieve more because those schools improve their skills, information, aspirations, and confidence? Or do these schools appear better mainly because families with more resources and higher-achieving students select into them? This project studies that question in Chile, focusing not only on test scores but also on students’ later higher-education choices: where they apply, what fields they choose, whether they enroll in STEM or other high-return programs, and how they progress after high school.

The question matters because field and program choice are economically consequential. A large literature shows that education affects earnings and mobility, but returns vary substantially across fields of study and institutions (Kirkeboen, Leuven, and Mogstad 2016). Students’ choices also respond to information, beliefs about earnings, perceived ability, preferences, and social context (Wiswall and Zafar 2015; Hastings, Neilson, and Zimmerman 2016). This means that schools may shape long-run outcomes even when they do not only raise measured academic achievement. A school might increase a student’s probability of applying to selective programs, taking the relevant entrance exams, considering STEM, understanding financial aid, or believing that a demanding field is feasible. These margins are especially important in Latin America, where inequality in information, academic preparation, and institutional support may shape postsecondary choices long before students submit college applications.

Estimating the causal effect of schools is difficult because students are not randomly assigned to schools. Families choose schools based on preferences, location, networks, resources, and expectations; schools also differ in peer composition and local context. As a result, simple comparisons across schools confound school effects with student selection. This is the core challenge in the school value-added literature. Existing work has made major progress using lotteries and quasi-random assignment to estimate effects on test scores, college enrollment, and other outcomes (Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff 2014; Angrist et al. 2016; Dobbie and Fryer 2015; Deming et al. 2014). However, much less is known about whether schools affect detailed higher-education choices, especially field choice, application portfolios, and the non-score mechanisms that lead students toward or away from particular paths.

Chile offers a uniquely powerful setting for answering this question. Recent reforms created a centralized school assignment system in which families submit ranked lists of schools. When schools are oversubscribed, seats are allocated using lottery tie-breakers within priority groups. Centralized assignment systems can turn school choice into a credible research design because students with similar preferences and priorities may receive different offers for reasons unrelated to their background or ability (Abdulkadiroğlu et al. 2017). Recent research also shows how assignment probabilities and school lotteries can be used to estimate and validate school value-added (Angrist et al. 2017; Angrist et al. 2024). Chile combines this assignment design with unusually rich administrative data, including school enrollment records, test scores, higher-education entrance exam outcomes, centralized applications to program-institution pairs, admissions information, enrollment, and subsequent academic trajectories.

My project uses these linked systems to estimate whether high schools causally affect students’ academic outcomes and higher-education choices. The empirical strategy begins by reconstructing the centralized assignment process and computing each student’s assignment risk: the probability that the student would be assigned to each school given their preferences, priorities, available seats, and lottery variation. This step is essential because Chile’s assignment system is not a simple textbook lottery. Institutional details such as quotas, priority groups, guaranteed admission in origin schools, and transition rules matter for determining which students are truly at risk of being assigned to different schools. I have already built the core assignment-probability machinery needed for this design.

The main analysis links lottery-generated school offers to later outcomes. I will compare students who applied through the centralized system and faced meaningful assignment risk, controlling for their assignment probabilities and predetermined characteristics. The outcomes include standardized math and language scores, exam-taking choices, application portfolios, STEM content of applications, field of enrollment, program-institution choice, and persistence or switching in higher education. In addition to estimating effects on these outcomes directly, I construct school-level measures of observational value added and ask whether lottery-induced movement toward schools with higher predicted value improves student outcomes. This provides a tractable way to study a large national system with many schools while preserving the central question: whether attending schools with stronger measured performance actually changes students’ later trajectories.

The proposed survey is necessary because administrative data alone cannot reveal the mechanisms behind these effects. Administrative records show where students apply and enroll, but not what they know, believe, expect, or feel when making those decisions. If a school increases STEM enrollment, for example, the mechanism could be improved math preparation, better information about earnings, stronger counseling, greater awareness of financial aid, exposure to role models, higher academic confidence, or a stronger sense of belonging in selective higher education. These mechanisms have different implications for policy. A test-score effect suggests one type of intervention; an information or confidence effect suggests another.

The grant would support a new survey of students entering and graduating from high school. Surveying students at both stages will allow me to measure differences in beliefs and information at school entry and near graduation. The instrument will collect information on knowledge of higher-education programs, expected earnings by field, perceived costs and financial aid, academic confidence, aspirations, sense of belonging, non-tested skills, exposure to counseling, and perceptions of school support. These measures are designed to complement, not replace, the administrative research design. By linking survey measures to school assignment and later administrative outcomes, I can ask whether schools that causally change higher-education choices also change the beliefs, information, confidence, or support that plausibly mediate those choices.

The academic contribution is threefold. First, the project extends the school-effects literature beyond conventional outcomes such as test scores and college enrollment, studying detailed application and field-choice outcomes at national scale. Second, it brings together two centralized systems--school assignment and higher-education applications--that are rarely observed jointly. This makes it possible to follow the path from high school assignment to program-level postsecondary choice with unusual granularity. Third, the survey will add mechanism data to a credible causal design, allowing the project to distinguish whether schools matter mainly through academic achievement or also through information, aspirations, perceived fit, and other non-score dimensions of decision-making.

The project is therefore both methodological and substantive. Methodologically, it uses centralized assignment to address selection into schools and to study school effectiveness in a setting where many schools, cohorts, and outcomes can be observed. Substantively, it asks how high schools shape the choices that determine students’ later specialization and opportunities. For Latin America, this is especially relevant because expanding access to education is not enough if students lack the information, preparation, or support needed to enter valuable fields and persist in them. The survey funded by this grant would make it possible to move from estimating whether schools matter to understanding how they matter.
