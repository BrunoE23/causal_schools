# How Much Do High Schools Matter? Causal Effects on Achievement and Major Choice

How much do high schools causally affect students' academic achievement and higher-education choices? In particular, do schools change not only test scores, but also the types of postsecondary programs to which students apply, the fields they choose, and their subsequent enrollment trajectories? 

Despite the centrality of these questions, the economics literature has surprisingly little evidence on schools' causal effects on detailed higher-education choices, especially at the level of field and program choice.

This paper answers these questions using administrative data from Chile, a setting that is well suited to studying school effects because both high school assignment and higher-education applications are centrally organized.

I exploit quasi-random variation from Chile's centralized high school assignment system, where lottery tie-breakers allocate seats in oversubscribed schools. I then link this variation to records from the centralized higher-education application system, which observe students' ranked applications to program-institution pairs, as well as enrollment outcomes. This combination makes it possible to study school effects on a richer set of outcomes than is typical in the literature, including standardized test scores, application portfolios, field choice, enrollment, and switching across programs or institutions.

The analysis is reduced-form. It estimates causal effects while controlling for assignment risk and predetermined covariates, and focuses on outcomes such as test scores, application portfolios, field choice, enrollment, and postsecondary trajectories. More broadly, the project argues that outcomes such as college enrollment are too coarse. By focusing on the content of students' higher-education choices, it aims to provide a richer account of how schools shape later specialization.
